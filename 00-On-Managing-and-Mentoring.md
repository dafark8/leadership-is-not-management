# On Managing and Mentoring
Above I mentioned the difference between management, leadership, and mentoring skills. There are a lot of different ways to define and distinguish among these. 

In my experience, **mentoring** is a formal or informal relationship of give and take between two people. There is mutual learning, trust, and sharing. **Mentorships are optional**. 

**Leadership** is earned. It is demonstrated through action and example. Taking the lead is, in my opinion, a misnomer as it implies you had a choice in claiming it. While you may be assigned *Lead* on a team, the respect that comes with leading is often one built by **trust and demonstrable behaviors**. 

**Management** is more **hierarchical** in nature, including operational skills. Great managers are also mentors and leaders though this is not always standard. Managers are more about prioritization, staffing, building and fostering trust and safe spaces, and allowing room to grow. Managers may not be directly responsible for an individual’s day to day tasks (though they are accountable for it). 

You may have many mentors. You may be the leader in some situations and defer to a leader in others. You (should) have only one manager. 

This writing is focussed primarily on tactical skills applicable to leadership and management. For simplicity, I will be referring to these skills as **Leadership Skills**, but know I mean these apply to both Leaders and Managers alike. If I use leadership or management (lowercase) know I am distinguishing and capitalized Leadership is inclusive of both. 

## Who is this for? 

This is for the leader and manager, new or seasoned, looking for tips, tricks, and actionable behaviors to adopt. As I work in the tech-space, the perspective is offered through this industry. I imagine the lessons are transferable to many other fields. 

There are tons of resources out there, a few personal favorites include Christina Wodtke’s [The Team that Managed Itself](https://a.co/d/0S3BuVf), Chris Avore and Russ Unger’s [Liftoff](https://rosenfeldmedia.com/books/ux-leadership/), and Kim Scott’s [Radical Candor](https://www.radicalcandor.com/) just to name a few. 

I take a lot of the lessons, themes, and attributes in those books and organize them into a quick guide that I try to lead by. This is not a replacement for those larger lessons or publications. This is my collection of lessons based on experience and bumps in the road. 

## How to Use this?

This book is divided into chapters based on the type of activity you will encounter as a leader. After reading the first two chapters, you may jump, or skip around based on what skill you are looking to practice.
