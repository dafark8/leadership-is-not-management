# Chapter 1 - Rome Wasn’t Built in a Day

My journey through Leadership has been a series of lessons. Yours will be too. Some lessons will be minor (I wish I had an agenda), or interpersonal (I realized so-and-so got steamrolled in a meeting, I need to remedy that next time). Others may have more measurable impacts (wow, someone asked to be put on a different team because of something I did, or didn’t, do). 

The key is to forgive of yourself. Forgive of others. Hopefully you are in an organization that supports individual growth and you have a manager who provides opportunities to learn, grow, and yes, fail. 

Borrowing a lesson from [Collaborative Improv](https://collaborativeimprov.wordpress.com/), accept feedback from a place of good intentions. Nobody tries to be a bad leader, and that includes you, So when mistakes happen, forgive yourself, identify a lesson, identify a path forward, and grow from there. 

## Delta File

One way to grow is through the Delta File. 

I don’t know if the [Manager-Tools website](https://www.manager-tools.com/2009/08/delta-file) is where I first learned about the Delta File, or if it is one of the more discoverable sources on the web. But the Delta File is a great tool for aspiring Leaders, or really humans in general. 

My definition of the Delta File is “the document that tracks your goals, motivations, and intent as a Leader.” A lot of what is in the Delta File are anti-patterns, as in things not to do. I try to turn these negatives into positives. For instance, early in my career I didn’t like how some managers were ‘all work and no play’. Rather than write “don’t be overly focussed on work” I wrote “remember to be human as a Leader and to ask (and actually care) about who is on your team, what they do, and what they are interested in”.  

This leads to another key part of my personal Delta File: **be genuine**. 

I, like many, can sniff out those doing the good-morning platitudes out of obligation, and those out of genuine care. Be the latter. It takes practice. Especially if you might not have much in common with someone. But that is when it is even more important to connect. 

A few excerpts from my personal delta file: 
- **Focus on your team as people.** What interests them. What do they do outside of work? What makes them humans and not just resources (a term I personally loathe.) 
I’ve had reports who make their own clothes, and another who is a certified yoga instructor. Two things I know nothing about but can be curious of. 
- **Always start with small talk.** Don’t jump right into work. And be genuine. If you don’t share your colleagues’ passion on a topic, ask them about it. Not only does it show interest, but you get to learn something. 
- **On time is early. Early is late. Late is dead.** This one is a life skill ingrained since I was little, and is pretty self-explanatory. 
- **When people leave your team, give them a gift.** All too often when people turn notice, or worse are laid off, the organization shifts to a “don’t let the door hit you on the way out” mentality. Don’t be that leader. While not perfect about the practice, I try to send a handwritten note and gift to folks as they leave the team. I find a nice pen set and journal are great for anyone. 
- **Be Genuine.** I won’t hide behind titles or roles. I’ll let you know when I’m confused or frustrated. I'll let you know when I’m hopeful. I manage expectations balancing the official company perspective with one of a human having human feelings. 
- **Be Transparent.** If I know something, and it is not privileged information or cause of concern, I try to share it as openly and candidly as I can. Leadership is not about knowing more than the team, it’s about being a conduit to the larger organization. That said, be mindful when sharing may lead to more confusion or uncertainty. 
- **Be Direct.** Nothing, in my opinion, is worse than platitudes and sugar coating a situation. **Clear is Kind.** I will call out your wins. I will be clear about growth opportunities. And when I have difficult news, I’ll rip the bandaid off (in a polite, clear, actionable and mindful manner). 
- **Trust.** I trust my team to be adults and get their job done. This is especially important with the popularity of distributed teams. Yes, I have expectations for what someone at your level should be able to accomplish. Be available for meetings, don’t ghost, and as long as you deliver quality work I trust you are balancing work- and personal-needs. 

Maybe a Delta File isn’t for you. A colleague has a presentation they share with their teams, and includes their guidelines for themselves and expectations for their team. Find what works for you. Be genuine and honest with yourself.
