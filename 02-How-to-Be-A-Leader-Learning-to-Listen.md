# Chapter 2 - Rome Wasn’t Built in a Day

It’s important to note, I have a UX Research background and mindset. So open ended questions, comfort-in-silence, and guiding conversations are skills I have honed over my career. One thing I find similar in my Research experience and my Leadership experience is the importance of shutting up and listening. 

*"If you shut up, let people talk, within three minutes they'll show you how crazy they really are."* - Errol Morris on [The Turnaround](https://maximumfun.org/episodes/the-turnaround-with-jesse-thorn/errol-morris/) with Jesse Thorn

Leadership is about **listening**. 
Leadership is about **supporting others** to solve their own problems. 
Leadership is not about having the answers, but **helping individuals find them**. 
And management is not about dictating the solution. 

When asked about my Leadership approach, I often describe it as a Socratic Method. By this, I mean I support my team to reason out their own solutions. I challenge them by asking “what outcome do you want to achieve,” “what expectations did you set with your teammates,” “what is the root of the problem” and of course “if you had unlimited resources or freedom, what solution would you propose”? 

We work backwards untangling what is reasonable, what is in their control, and lastly what I can do to support their progress. 

I listen to what they need. I let them explore. Only then do I offer solutions or actions. Ultimately, the path is theirs. As a Leader, I encourage folks to explore options. As a manager, I have more agency to “choose for” my team. I urge you against that. Unless you see your team member doing something potentially dangerous (to themselves, the team, the organization) measuring “my way would have been better” is an exercise in what-ifs. Encourage new ways. Challenge your assumptions and work towards a holistic perspective. Good Leaders don’t need to be right. They need to see the progress and success of their teams. 


## Sounding Board

Most people know the answer, they just need to externalize it. This is the idea behind rubber duck debugging. Being a sounding board is asking what is the problem, and listening. It’s about probing where appropriate, and letting the conversation breath when necessary. 

A good Leader is equal parts therapist and counselor. Let the emotions out and guide the individual to their own self-discovery. 

Only after the individual’s “aha” moment, should Leaders offer their perspective. And in offering it, realize that individual mileage may vary. 


## Build Bridges

Oftentimes, I find teams frustrated that they lack the agency, or lack the introductions to impact change themselves. 

It takes a lot to ask for help, and often I find people want to solve challenges on their own. (As an aside, I tell my team that asking for help is the greatest strength you can demonstrate.) Your role as a Leader is to know who can support your team, make the introductions, and get out of the way. See my thoughts on Random 1:1s as a means to building bridges. 

Some of my favorite experiences as a Leader have been encouraging individual practitioners to reach out to senior stakeholders, product owners, and even executive leadership to talk directly about their interests, needs, and motivations. I’ve witnessed team members’ surprise “I can talk directly to them?” 

There is a history of gatekeeping across organizations and some of the greatest progress, personal and professional, comes by getting out of the way and letting your team thrive. 

## Shield
When I first became a manager, I was gifted a brown coffee mug with two simple emojis on it: The poop emoji and an umbrella emoji. 

Because as a manager, that’s what you are. A poop-umbrella. Or more kindly put, a shield. You protect your team from changing priorities. You protect from uncontrollable product managers or technical leads. You take the (proverbial) bullet and raise them up as heroes. 

It is worth saying this in no uncertain terms: If you are not comfortable asking the hard questions to protect your team, maybe management isn’t for you. 

As I am writing about Leadership holistically, leaders protect their team, but the stakes are lower. Leaders have managers for escalations. For managers, you are the point of escalation. 


## So Why do you Want to Manage?

Focussing specifically on management, it’s important to pursue this path with clear understanding of your motivations. So I want to ask: 

*Why do you want to be a manager? What drives you?* 

Everyone has their own motivations, and I would never be able to capture them all here. A few to be particularly mindful of: 

**Do you want to manage because you want to be in control?** This is a bad reason to be a manager. Everyone has a boss. And wanting to be in control does not make a good manager. And being a manager does not give you control. 

**Do you want to manage because you want to lead decisions?** Managers facilitate decisions. But oftentimes are not the sole decision maker. See above about control. I know plenty of individual practitioners with more decision making agency than their managers. 

**Do you want to manage for career growth?** Growing into management used to be the sole path for career growth. In some organizations it still may be the primary path. However, growing into management requires two key things: 
1. Organizational growth 
1. Financial investment. 

By this, I mean you cannot be promoted to manager if nobody junior is being hired to manage. So either you need to see rapid team growth or existing managers need to transition to other roles leaving vacancies / opportunities. Fortunately, the industry is shifting and individual practitioner paths, often called principle tracks are increasing in their opportunity for professional acceleration. 

**Do you want to manage because you are tired of making?** As mentioned about career growth the tech industry is seeing a trend of practitioner-track and management-track career paths. If you want to be part of the process, but may have outgrown the “pixel pushing” or craft equivilan, maybe management is for you. If you want more agency but enjoy the making, look at leadership roles. 

### Why do I manage? 
I'm writing about leadership and management, and it would only be fair to share my own motivations for the career path I have been pursuing. 

**I manage to grow the future of the industry.** I am excited to empower others in their personal and career growth. I see it as my role as a Leader to help you find your next role. Hopefully in our team, or in our org. If that means elsewhere in the industry I hope to have that conversation too. 

Selfishly, I manage to inspire future managers and, if I am lucky, train and coach someone who may one day be my boss. What a better opportunity to know I will be happy working for someone then training them in how I work! 

## How Do You Become a Leader? 

So how do you become a leader? It sounds silly, but start leading! 

*"Demonstrate leadership beyond the job title. Become a trusted source for domain knowledge, design practice, and diplomatic communication. Be someone people contact first for opinion, advice, and clarity."* - Dylan Willbanks 

Dylan Willbanks provides the insight above, and this captures a lot of how Leaders Lead. Demonstrate your skill and mastery of craft. Be available. Be trusted. Be the name that comes to mind when people think of a problem you have an aptitude to solve. 

Adding my own thoughts, nobody is “owed” a leadership position. Promotion and elevation comes by demonstrating not only “why you” but also the business value of putting you in a leadership position. 

How is your leadership more valuable than your production? How do you multiply the contributions of others? 

From there, you can explore conversations about leadership roles and management roles. What is interesting to you? What motivates you? 

And keep in mind, growth through leadership takes on many forms - mentoring, project leading, skill based, and recognition comes in many forms. 

## Where Do I Find Leadership Roles?  

I hope it is clear Leadership comes in many shapes and sizes. Principal/Lead roles are often individual practitioner roles with mentorship and project-leadership responsibilities. Manager or Director roles often have more business development and/or people management responsibilities. 

We may also find our skills hidden in other titles. For instance a lot of stakeholder management, roadmapping, and defining the needs, skills often thought of as Research skills, are starting to shift to more Product Management (different use of the word Manage there) roles. And with the influx of artificial intellgence tools and roles, being user-intelligence-officers, data-modelers, or agentic-partners are all potential roles and job titles I have seen. 


### How do you know what role is right for you? 

Start with some self reflection. **What role do you want?** Or as I often say about the products and services I work on, **what do you want to be when you grow up?**

What do you want to be as a Leader? 

What type of role do you want? 

Do you want the IC path? The manager path? 

Rather than try and answer this off the cuff, try putting together your own job description. 

I have had the privilege of writing my own job description on at least two occasions, and then being hired with those specifications in mind. In other instances, my role has changed and I’ve collaborated with leaders on refining my existing job description. 

Rather than reinvent the wheel on job profiles, I’ll refer you to [Liftoff](https://rosenfeldmedia.com/books/ux-leadership/) which has some solid examples and frameworks.

### Why Write a Job Description? 
There are a few benefits to writing your own job description, whether you are a job seeker or currently employed. 

**For Job Seekers**, writing your own job description allows you to sniff-test potential job opportunities against your own motivations and desires. It keeps you honest, applying to roles you are both a fit for and aspire to do. 

**For those employed**, writing your own job description helps internalize your organization’s expectations of you. As expectations change, you can refer back to the job description and work with your manager to discuss what should be added, changed, or removed in your role. This is an opportunity to use the JD as a baseline for setting team and organizational goals, often a metric that informs individual performance and bonuses come end of the year. 

In either case, writing your job description is a way to externalize your accomplishments and value to a potential, or current employer. 

### When to Write a Job Description?

As mentioned, you can write your job description if you are looking for a job or currently employed. Don’t write it once and file the JD away. Write it, and be intentional about revisiting the description at least annually (if not more often) to see how the business, the market, and your own interests change. This, like everything in this writing, should be a living document.
