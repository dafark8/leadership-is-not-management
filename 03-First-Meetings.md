# Chapter 3 Inaugural Meetings

Team meetings and individual meetings are equally important to building rapport, trust, and knowing what’s going on as a Leader. There are various styles and approaches to meeting and everyone should find their own voice. Generally the following meeting types are good foundations for any Leadership style. 

## Inaugural 1:1 Meeting

Inaugural 1:1s have value in any instance for any role. They are not exclusive to managers. They are not exclusive to leaders. **They are not about business.** They are about getting to know one another. Whether starting a new job, or for internal transfers joining a new team, onboarding is overwhelming. I break the inaugural 1:1 into different meeting types. 

### The welcome 1:1

A half hour, specific for managers on a team member's first day. It is a half hour and generally scheduled after the HR onboarding, before the bombardment of emails and invites happen. I check-in on onboarding, getting set up, and if they have any questions. I want to offer a friendly face in the sea of paperwork and procedures. 

### The settling in 1:1 

The second manager 1:1 is an hour, and focussed on understanding who the team member is, their work style, and their expectations of the job, and of me as their leader. Some of this may be duplicative from the interview process but now that they have the job, there is a greater likelihood of honesty and retention. 

Getting this level of personal information in an early conversation is not easy. I credit Elayna Spratley, a colleague from my time with Cisco, for introducing me to the Today and Beyond framework. Shared at the first 30-minute 1:1, team members have a few days to reflect and fill out the questionnaire. Then, during the 1-hour session, we can review our answers and discuss similarities and differences. 

It is crucial that you, as a Leader, prefill the answers for your own reflection. This demonstrates both the level of detail expected for a conversation and shows your own vulnerability as a Leader to your team. The idea isn’t to set up a hierarchy “tell me what you need” but a working relationship “how we can support one another”. 

As an added bonus, I leverage the Today and Beyond information for the team charter. 

### The meet n' greet 1:1

Not specific to a manager, or a leader, I also want to acknowledge meet n greet 1:1s. These are with a new team member's peers, stakeholders, and partners. These span well into the first 90-days and are intended as social "hi, im new here, how can I help". I describe more on various 1:1s in Chapter 4 and want to highlight these here. Leadership is not top-down and building these bridges as individuals is part of my growth. 

## Team Kickoff: Team Charter, or Team Norms
What is a Team Charter? 
Where inaugural 1:1s may err more strongly towards a manager role, Team Charters or Team Norms documents, set expectations of when and how we collaborate with one another as a team. This is great for anyone in a Leadership role. When I led product teams we had one for the project cadence. When I managed designers we had one set of norms around critiques, support, and collaboration. Managing researchers, the core tenets stay the same but the execution changes. 

### Who is a team charter for? 

A team charter is for me and the team I directly Lead. We are the only ones held accountable by this charter. The larger department we work with has their own charter, and the organization as a whole has its own norms. We can only influence our small circle and lead by example. 

### Why does it matter? 

Team Charters allow us to know when and how to engage with one another. What are our core working hours? How do we treat email, messages, or meetings? What is the bare minimum we expect of one another to “show up”? 
### Using a Team Charter

Team charters are living documents. They should not be set and forgotten. They should be revisited quarterly in the first year, and bi-annually moving forward. 
### Team Charter Framework 

As I mentioned, I leverage the Today and Beyond framework to seed the Team Charter exercise. Beyond that, I set aside 1.5-2 hours for a (virtual) workshop completing the Team Charter framework found here. I usually ask about 30-minutes of prep time for individuals. 



After the collaborative experience, I summarize the norms into a more scannable format and post in either the wiki or another shared team repository. 

### Other meetings 
As a leader, your calendar is filled with meetings. 1:1 meetings, Stakeholder meetings, planning, review, and the dreaded fire-drill meetings. All of these are important. 

It is important to start with inaugural 1:1 meetings and team charters, or norms, as these are the foundation for our working relationship with our peers. From there, we can have recurring rituals and conversations. 

But it starts with trust. 

It starts with putting ourselves out there.
